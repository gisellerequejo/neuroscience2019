Orofacial Functions: From Neural Mechanisms to Rehabilitation









5th Annual sFN Satellite Event

18 October, 2019. 355 East Erie - Chicago, IL 60611

The Conference

Orofacial functions (e.g., chewing, swallowing, speaking) are complex, life-sustaining sensorimotor behaviors controlled by the central and peripheral nervous systems. These functions are impaired in many neurological diseases or following injury, with no age or gender boundaries. The symposium will provide insights into latest discoveries in neural mechanisms underlying orofacial functions and rehabilitation in humans and animals.

This 5th symposium brings together a diverse group of speakers whose research focuses on orofacial behaviors in health and disease. The program includes sessions on Feeding, Swallowing, and Rehabilitation featuring from basic science to treatments for neurological diseases resulting in orofacial symptoms.

Nick Ramsey

    The Brain Function research team at the University Medical Center of Utrecht conducts studies on the representation of movement and language using electrocorticography and 7 Tesla functional MRI. The primary line of research aims to develop implantable Brain-Computer Interface (BCI) solutions for people with locked-in Syndrome (LIS). We aim to translate neuroscientific knowledge to solutions that offer LIS patients a new means of communication that works in daily life. This requires a focus on robust and reliable performance. Success was achieved in 2016 when we showed that the first LIS patient implanted with a fully implanted BCI system could use it at home (Vansteensel ea NEJM 2016). We are currently working on increasing versatility from the current ‘brainclick’ to ‘point-and-click’ capability, and on decoding elements of covert speech from sensorimotor cortex.

Yingying Wang

    The cortical representation of orofacial pneumotactile stimulation involves a complex network. This cortical network is important for movement production and regulation, and possibly recovering lost function after stroke. However, this cortical network is unknown. The present study aims to identify the cortical network during unilateral orofacial pneumotactile stimulation with various velocity settings (5, 25, 65 cm/s) using functional magnetic resonance imaging. 21 neurotypical adults were studied and functional connectivity was examined using region of interest (ROI) based approach. The left primary and secondary somatosensory cortices were chosen as sources, and 30 other ROIs were chosen as targets based on our previous findings and literature. Our results show more functional connectivity in the primary somatosensory cortex than the secondary somatosensory cortex for all velocity settings. The 65 cm/s elicited the most connections in the contralateral hemisphere among all velocity settings. The difference of functional cortical network among three velocity settings may indicate the optimal stimulation setting for better therapeutic effects on stroke recovery. Yingying Wang (UNL): Dr. Yingying Wang, joined the University of Nebraska-Lincoln faculty in 2016 as an Assistant Professor in the Department of Special Education and Communication Disorders after her two-year postdoc fellowship in Boston Children’s Hospital/Harvard Medical School. Her expertise lies in multimodal neuroimaging techniques including fMRI, DTI, MEG, fNRIs and she is using these techniques to understand the neural circuits facilitating reading development and study brain plasticity due to various sensory, auditory stimulations. Her webpage: https://www.thewanglab.com

David Reed

    Neuron/glial antigen 2 in temporomandibular joint health and disease. The most common joint pathology affecting the temporomandibular joint (TMJ) is osteoarthritis (OA). While the etiology of TMJ OA has yet to be fully resolved, mechanical overloading of the articular fibrocartilage is a key initiating factor. The tissue’s response to mechanical loading is mediated in part through the biophysical and biochemical microenvironment of the extracellular matrix surrounding the cell. We seek to identify select and specific cell-matrix interactions that are responsive to mechanical loading to identify novel therapeutic targets for the early diagnosis and management of TMJ OA pain and dysfunction. To characterize changes in the extracellular microenvironment during TMJ OA, we used a unilateral partial discectomy surgical instability preclinical animal model. To characterize changes in the extracellular matrix, tissue samples were imaged with a two photon fluorescence and second harmonic generation microscope. The superficial layer of the mandibular condylar cartilage was dramatically remodeled during the early stages of TMJ OA, changing the organization, orientation, and composition of collagen types I, III and VI. We then evaluated the spatiotemporal distribution of a cell receptor of type VI collagen, Neuron/glial antigen 2 (NG2). The localized depletion of type VI collagen was associated with the translocation of NG2 from the cell membrane to the cytosol. Further, cytosolic NG2 was found to colocalized with markers of clathrin mediated endocytosis. To determine if NG2 was internalized in a load dependent manner, isolated mandibular chondrocytes were exposed to mechanical strain in a custom compression bioreactor. Cells were loaded under static and cyclic compression. Cells loaded statically were found to have substantially higher levels of the NG2 intracellular domain within the cytosol. The NG2 intracellular domain has a hypothesized role as a transcriptional regulator that is activated by cleavage of its ectodomain to induce nuclear translocation. Taken together, these data illustrate that mechanical stress may disrupt cell-matrix interactions and potentially impact cell behavior through NG2 mediated pathways within mandibular chondroctyes. Lab: Our lab focuses on understanding how chondrocyte-extracellular matrix interactions regulate homeostasis in the cells of the temporomandibular joint (TMJ) and how changes in the biophysical and biochemical microenvironment of the cell impact the progression of degenerative disorders like osteoarthritis (OA). The long-term goal of our research group is to advance the TMJ OA translational pipeline and address clinical problems associated with the classification, prevention, and treatment of TMJ dysfunction. Lab website: https://reedd.people.uic.edu/

Man-Kyo Chung

    Measurement of orofacial pain by assessing facial grimace and voluntary biting in mice. Injury or inflammation in orofacial tissues produces acute and chronic pain. Orofacial pain involves intricate nociceptive processing in trigeminal pain pathways, which affects voluntary and involuntary changes in orofacial motor functions. Molecular and neural mechanisms of orofacial pain have been widely studied based on preclinical rodent models mimicking the sources and outcomes of pain. In rodents, orofacial pain has been evaluated in multiple ways. Measurement of evoked pain by the assessment of nocifensive behaviors responding to chemical or mechanical stimuli has been well established. In contrast, measurement of non-evoked pain in rodent model has long been challenging. Recently, we evaluated the effectiveness of adopting two outcomes involving altered orofacial motor functions as measurements of orofacial pain. First, to evaluate the extent of non-evoked spontaneous pain changes, facial grimace was assessed by evaluating characteristic facial expressions. Second, voluntary bite force change has been assessed to evaluate pain level on biting. We tested these two non-evoked spontaneous or functional pain measurements in multiple rodent orofacial pain conditions derived from multiple sources of injuries, such as masseter inflammation, masseter tendon ligation, TMJ inflammation or orthodontic tooth movement. We found that changes in facial grimace or bite force commonly reported pain levels with distinct time courses based on the severity of injury. Pain transmission through trigeminal ascending pain pathway is necessary for producing these behavioral changes since chemical ablation or chemogenetic inhibition of major trigeminal pain circuit attenuated inflammation-induced changes in facial grimace and bite force. Orofacial pain transmission should influence orofacial motor regulations at multiple levels of the ascending and descending pain pathways. Especially, the brainstem is a well-known structure for broad sensory motor interactions responsible for various oral motor functions. It would be interesting to determine the key intersections between pain and motor regulation, which should help for better rehabilitation of pain-related abnormalities of oral motor functions. Bio:I am a dentist scientist investigating the molecular mechanisms of trigeminal pain focused on the roles of nociceptors and nociceptive molecules. I have been investigating the electrophysiological and biophysical properties of temperature-gated transient receptor potential (TRP) channels from the context of thermosensation and pain. I am expert in cellular physiological analysis of heat-gated ion channels and study the function of TRP channels at sub-receptor level through the combination of mutagenic and electrophysiological approach. Molecular physiological analysis of TRPV1 is further extended to the animal studies in vivo and human genetics analysis. Our lab has been investigating the contribution of phosphorylation and genetic modification of TRPV1 to craniofacial muscle pain. https://www.dental.umaryland.edu/neuralpain/faculty-and-staff/dr-chung/

Valerie Hamilton

    Clinical and Videofluoroscopic Characteristics of Swallow and Preliminary Effects of Respiratory Strengthening Treatment in Post-Traumatic Spinal Cord Injury. Dysphagia following traumatic cervical spinal cord injury is understudied and clinicians are left with little evidence to guide their clinical evaluations and management decisions. However, recent evidence is growing for both assessment measures and treatment techniques aimed at restoring function and preventing dysphagia-related consequences. The presentation will aim to inform researchers and clinicians of emerging evidence regarding salient characteristics of videofluoroscopic traumatic cervical spinal cord injury profiles, limitations of clinical swallow evaluations, including measures of lingual and respiratory strength, and applications of respiratory strengthening in this population based on recent pilot data. Valerie Hamilton M.A., CCC-SLP oversees speech-language pathologists and occupational therapists in the Think and Speak Lab, which specializes in treating aphasia, dysphagia, and impairments in vision and cognition. She has been with Shirley Ryan AbilityLab since 2012 and works predominantly with neurologically impaired patients specializing in dysphagia management. Her research has focused on advancing the assessment and treatment of dysphagia in spinal cord injury, Parkinson’s disease, stroke, and traumatic brain injury.
